HVAC Service Technician — Experience Scoring Framework
This scoring system is designed for your HVAC Recruiting Tool.
It evaluates candidates even if their title isn't exactly "HVAC Service Technician" by using transferable-skills logic, equivalent roles, and binary requirements.

1. Hard Requirements (Binary Filters)
If the job post defines hard requirements (ex: EPA Certification, 2+ years experience, ability to work on residential systems), the candidate must satisfy these through:
- Direct HVAC Service Technician experience
- OR Equivalent HVAC field roles that perform similar service/diagnostic duties, such as:
  - HVAC Maintenance Technician (intermediate/senior)
  - HVAC Installer with service crossover
  - Refrigeration Technician (with HVAC overlap)
  - Building Engineer with heavy HVAC workload
  - HVAC Apprentice (ONLY when the job accepts entry-level / "1 year experience" roles)

If the candidate does not meet the binary minimums, they fail the hard filter and should not be scored further.

2. Core Competency Categories
Claude should score candidates using the following four service-focused competency groups.
Strong Service Technician candidates typically show 3–4 categories.
Mid-level candidates show 2–3.
Entry-level or weak candidates show 0–1.

A. HVAC Diagnostics & Problem Solving
Look for indicators such as:
- Diagnosing common HVAC failures (capacitors, contactors, boards, motors)
- Troubleshooting cooling/heating issues
- Electrical diagnostic skills
- Experience with minisplits, heat pumps, gas furnaces
- Ability to run performance tests
- Basic refrigeration cycle understanding
- Handling service calls independently
This category should trigger even if the job title doesn't explicitly mention "service."

B. Installation, Repair & Maintenance Skills
Evidence of hands-on technical abilities, including:
- Performing maintenance: cleaning coils, checking pressures, inspecting systems
- Completing component-level repairs
- Changing motors, compressors, or control boards
- Brazing, soldering, line set work
- Performing partial installations or assisting on full installs
- Knowledge of local code and safety standards
This category is fundamental to any service technician.

C. Customer Interaction & Field Independence
Candidates score higher if they show:
- Experience interacting with customers on-site
- Ability to explain repairs or system issues
- Working independently in the field
- Providing repair recommendations
- Writing accurate service notes or invoices
- Coordinating with dispatch or supervisors
This helps distinguish true field-ready techs from purely shop-based or helper roles.

D. Reliability, Professionalism & Job Management
Includes indicators like:
- Managing daily service routes
- Consistency in completing assigned calls
- On-time arrival and reliable scheduling
- Maintaining service records
- Strong communication and accountability
- Following company procedures
These indirectly show service-readiness and are often mentioned in resumes.

3. Acceptable Equivalent Job Titles
Claude should recognize the following roles as valid or partially valid for HVAC Service Technician evaluation even if the title doesn't explicitly include "Service."

Strong Equivalents (Automatically High Relevance):
- HVAC Maintenance Technician
- HVAC Installer (Level II or higher)
- HVAC Technician (generic)
- HVAC Field Technician
- HVAC Repair Technician
- Refrigeration Technician (with HVAC crossover)
- Building Engineer (HVAC-heavy roles)
- Facilities Technician (HVAC-heavy)
- Residential or Light Commercial HVAC Technician
- Apprentice HVAC Technician (only for early-career roles)

Moderate Equivalents (Context-dependent based on job description):
- Lead Installer (if the job allows installation-heavy backgrounds)
- General Maintenance Technician (must show real HVAC experience)
- Handyman roles (only if they explicitly include HVAC systems)
- Property Maintenance Techs (with HVAC servicing duties)

Claude should use semantic reasoning to decide whether the role functions like a service job even if the wording is indirect.

===================================================================================================
SCORING DEFINITIONS
===================================================================================================

Rules:
- Experience is the most important factor
- Required experience: candidate has exact or more experience than required amount
- Close to required experience: candidate has 50-95% of required experience
- Not close to required experience: candidate has under 50% of required experience
- Good resume: proper formatting, no typos, proper punctuation/grammar, at least 2 substantive bullets per experience
- Mid resume: professional formatting with few grammatical errors, but missing key elements like bullets under experiences, or has limited bullets, or has small formatting issues
- Poor resume: multiple punctuation errors, multiple typos, numerous formatting issues, AND lacks substantive content
- No work gap: under 6 months of unemployment
- Small work gap: 6 months to 1 year of unemployment
- Large work gap: over 1 year of unemployment
- Job hoppy: frequent job changes (significant negative factor)
- Distance: <30 miles (minimal impact), 30-50 miles (moderate impact), >50 miles (major impact)
- Certifications listed always scores higher than certifications not listed
